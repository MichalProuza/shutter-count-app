Does Video Recording
Affect Shutter Count?

Short answer: no. Video recording uses the electronic shutter — no mechanical parts move, and nothing is added to the shutter counter. Here's the full explanation for hybrid and video-first shooters.

Why Video Doesn't Affect the Shutter Counter

When your camera records video, the sensor reads its pixels continuously in a rolling scan — frame by frame, typically at 24, 30, or 60 frames per second. This readout is entirely electronic: there is no physical curtain opening and closing between frames.

The mechanical shutter — the precision curtain assembly that the shutter count tracks — is parked open during video. The sensor is exposed continuously through the open curtain, with exposure controlled by the sensor's electronic readout timing, not by curtain movement.

Since the curtain mechanism doesn't move during video, the shutter counter does not increment. You can record hours of footage without adding a single actuation to your count.

Practical implication: A camera used heavily for video work will have a much lower shutter count relative to its actual age and usage than a stills-only camera. Low shutter count alone does not mean a camera is "barely used" if it was a video workhorse.

What Does and Doesn't Increment the Shutter Count?

The mechanical shutter counter only tracks events that cause physical curtain movement:

Action Adds to Shutter Count? Why
Mechanical shutter photo Yes — +1 Both curtains cycle physically
EFCS photo (Electronic Front Curtain) Yes — +1 Rear curtain still moves mechanically
Electronic shutter (silent) photo No No mechanical curtain movement
Video recording No Electronic sensor readout; shutter stays open
Live View display No Continuous electronic readout, no actuation
Autofocus operation No AF uses sensor or phase-detect; no shutter movement
Still photo taken during video Depends on model Some use electronic shutter; some use mechanical

What About Cameras Used Primarily for Video?

Cameras like the Sony FX3, Sony A7S III, Canon EOS R5 C, and Nikon Z6 III are popular for professional video work. A camera used primarily for video can easily accumulate hundreds of hours of recording while maintaining a very low shutter count.

Evaluating Video-First Cameras in the Used Market

When buying a used camera that was primarily used for video, shutter count tells only part of the story. Equally important factors for video-heavy cameras include:

  • Thermal wear: Extended video recording generates significant heat. Cameras with history of long recording sessions may have aged heat-related components (capacitors, thermal pads) faster than shutter count suggests.
  • Card slot wear: Heavy video use means more frequent card writes. CFexpress and SD card slots are generally durable, but heavy use is worth noting.
  • Port wear: HDMI, USB-C, and XLR ports on video cameras can show wear from frequent cable connection.
  • Autofocus motor wear: Continuous AF during long video clips can wear the lens and body AF components.
  • General cosmetic wear: Grip rubber, button wear, and body condition give independent clues about how heavily a camera was used.
Buyer advice: Don't dismiss a video camera as heavily used just because it has a low shutter count. And don't assume a video-heavy camera is in pristine condition because the shutter count is low. Evaluate holistically.

Does Live View Affect Shutter Count?

Live View operation itself does not increment the shutter count. While Live View is active, the camera reads the sensor electronically and displays the feed without mechanical curtain movement.

However, taking a still photo while in Live View does fire the mechanical shutter on most cameras (unless you've enabled electronic shutter / silent mode). The Live View display time accumulates no actuations; only the moment you press the shutter release to capture a photo adds 1 to the count.

On mirrorless cameras, Live View is the default shooting mode — the viewfinder and rear screen are always showing a live electronic feed. All mirrorless cameras effectively "use Live View" for all shooting, which is one reason their shutter mechanisms are simpler than DSLRs.

Practical Advice for Hybrid Shooters

If you shoot both stills and video:

  • Your shutter count reflects only stills taken with the mechanical or EFCS shutter. It doesn't reflect your total camera usage time.
  • For high-volume still bursts, consider switching to electronic shutter mode during video intervals to avoid accumulating actuations during setup shots.
  • When selling your camera, be transparent about its video use history — a low shutter count on a video workhorse doesn't fully represent the camera's usage.
  • When buying, ask whether the camera was used primarily for stills or video — then check the shutter count in context of the answer.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the camera record shutter count somewhere for video?

No. There is no separate "video recording time" counter embedded in the camera's accessible metadata. The shutter counter only tracks mechanical actuations. Some professional cinema cameras track total power-on time or rolling time, but this is not a standard feature on mirrorless or DSLR cameras.

I shot thousands of video clips but my shutter count seems very low. Is something wrong?

No — this is expected. Video does not increment the counter. Your low count is accurate. If you want to verify the count is being read correctly, take a fresh mechanical-shutter photo and drop it into shuttercount.app to confirm the reading matches your expectations based on your stills shooting history.

Does recording a timelapse (using interval shooting) affect shutter count?

Yes. Timelapse sequences taken with the mechanical shutter add 1 actuation per frame. A timelapse of 3,000 frames = 3,000 actuations. If your camera supports in-camera timelapse using electronic shutter, you can avoid this. Otherwise, timelapse can accumulate shutter count faster than regular shooting.

Do Canon / Nikon / Sony / Fujifilm cameras all handle video + shutter count the same way?

Yes — all modern mirrorless cameras from these brands use the electronic shutter for video recording and do not increment the mechanical shutter counter during video. The specific camera model affects how stills-during-video work (electronic vs. mechanical), but video recording itself is universally electronic across these platforms.
