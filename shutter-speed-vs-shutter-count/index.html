Shutter Speed vs Shutter Count:
What’s the Difference?

Two camera terms, one confusing word. Shutter speed and shutter count are completely unrelated — one is an exposure setting you change per photo, the other is a lifetime wear counter for your camera.

What Is Shutter Speed?

Shutter speed — more precisely called exposure time — is the length of time the camera's shutter remains open during a single photograph. It is one of the three pillars of exposure alongside aperture and ISO.

How It Works

  • A fast shutter speed (e.g. 1/2000 s) freezes motion — used for sports, birds in flight, water droplets.
  • A slow shutter speed (e.g. 1/30 s or longer) blurs motion — used for waterfalls, light trails, low-light photography.
  • Each doubling of shutter speed halves the light entering the sensor; each halving doubles it.

How to Change It

In Manual (M) or Shutter Priority (Tv/S) mode, you set the shutter speed directly using the command dial. In Auto and Aperture Priority modes, the camera selects it based on your aperture, ISO, and metering reading.

Where It's Recorded

Shutter speed is recorded in every image's EXIF data as the ExposureTime tag. You can see it in Lightroom, your camera's playback info screen, or any EXIF reader.

What Is Shutter Count?

Shutter count — also called shutter actuations or the actuation count — is the total number of times the camera's mechanical shutter has fired since the camera was manufactured. It's a cumulative, ever-increasing number stored in the camera's internal hardware and written into each RAW file's metadata.

How It Works

  • Every photo taken with the mechanical shutter increments the counter by 1 — regardless of shutter speed.
  • A photo at 1/8000 s and a 30-second long exposure each add exactly 1 actuation.
  • Photos taken with the fully electronic shutter (silent mode) do not increment the counter, because no mechanical parts move.
  • Video recording does not increment the counter.

Why It Matters

The mechanical shutter is a precision component with a finite rated lifespan (typically 100,000–500,000 actuations depending on camera tier). A high shutter count signals more wear, which is particularly relevant when buying or selling a used camera — similar to checking mileage on a car.

Where It's Stored

Shutter count is stored in the MakerNote section of the EXIF data inside each RAW file — a proprietary block used by each manufacturer (Canon, Nikon, Sony, Fujifilm) to store camera-specific data not covered by the standard EXIF spec.

How Are They Related?

Shutter speed and shutter count are connected in one way: every mechanical-shutter photo adds 1 to the count, regardless of the shutter speed used. That's the only relationship.

A photographer who mostly shoots long-exposure night photography at 30 seconds will accumulate shutter count just as fast as one shooting at 1/4000 s — if they take the same number of photos. The speed of the shutter does not cause more or less wear per actuation.

Common misconception: “I shoot with slow shutter speeds, so my shutter must wear out faster.” This is not true. Each actuation — fast or slow — is one cycle of the curtain mechanism. The rated lifespan is measured in cycles, not in total time the curtain was open.

How to Check Each

Checking Shutter Speed

Shutter speed is visible in:

  • Your camera's viewfinder or LCD display while shooting.
  • The EXIF data of any photo (ExposureTime tag) — readable in Lightroom, Preview, Windows File Explorer properties, or any EXIF viewer.
  • The camera's image review playback screen (press Info/Display to cycle through data overlays).

Checking Shutter Count

Shutter count requires a dedicated tool because it's stored in the proprietary MakerNote section:

  1. Take a photo with the mechanical shutter (not silent/electronic mode).
  2. Transfer the original RAW file to your computer — don't convert or edit it.
  3. Drop it into shuttercount.app — free, no upload, runs in your browser.
  4. Your exact actuation count appears instantly alongside a remaining life estimate.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a very fast shutter speed (1/8000 s) wear out the shutter more than 1/60 s?

No. Each actuation counts as one regardless of the shutter speed. The mechanical curtain completes one full travel cycle per shot at any speed — though at fast speeds, both curtains travel simultaneously rather than one after the other.

I changed my shutter speed setting — did that add to the shutter count?

No. Only pressing the shutter button and taking a photo increments the count. Changing settings, half-pressing to focus, reviewing photos in playback, or adjusting any menu options do not affect the shutter count.

Can the shutter count ever go down?

Only after an authorised manufacturer service centre replaces the shutter mechanism and resets the counter to zero. No setting, firmware update, or file editing can decrease the internal hardware counter.

Is there a "good" shutter speed for preserving shutter life?

No specific shutter speed preserves shutter life better than another. If you want to reduce shutter count accumulation, use electronic shutter (silent mode) when conditions allow — electronic shots do not increment the counter at all.
